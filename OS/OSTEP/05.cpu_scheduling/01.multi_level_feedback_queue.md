# OSTEP — CPU Scheduling: Multi-Level Feedback Queue (Ch. 8)

> *Based on notes from Operating Systems: Three Easy Pieces — Scheduling: The Multi-Level Feedback Queue*

> **Big picture**
> MLFQ does not demand **a priori** knowledge of a job's nature. It **observes**
> execution and prioritizes accordingly — learning from **recent behavior**
> (feedback) instead of requiring known run times upfront.
> It aims for the **best of both worlds**: excellent performance (similar to
> SJF/STCF) for short-running interactive jobs, and fairness with steady
> progress for long-running CPU-intensive workloads.
> Basic policies from [00.scheduling_introduction.md](./00.scheduling_introduction.md):
> STCF for turnaround, RR for fairness/response — MLFQ combines both ideas.

## 1. One-page cheat sheet

| Topic | Meaning | Remember this |
| --- | --- | --- |
| MLFQ | Multiple priority queues + feedback to adjust priority. | History guides the future; no need to know job length upfront. |
| Allotment | CPU time allowed at one priority level before demotion. | Initially assume allotment = one time slice. |
| Rule 1 | Higher priority runs first. | `Priority(A) > Priority(B)` → A runs, B doesn't. |
| Rule 2 | Same priority → RR. | `Priority(A) = Priority(B)` → A and B run in round robin. |
| Rule 3 | New jobs start at top. | Assume short/interactive until proven otherwise. |
| Rule 4a / 4b | Original demotion rules. | 4a: use full allotment → demote. 4b: I/O before allotment → stay, reset allotment. |
| Rule 5 | Periodic priority boost. | Every period S, move all jobs to top queue — fights starvation and behavior change. |
| Rule 4 (revised) | Better accounting. | Demote after total allotment used at a level, **regardless of how many times** the job yielded. |
| Gaming | Trick scheduler for extra CPU. | Issue I/O just before allotment ends (e.g. at 99%) to stay high priority — fixed by revised Rule 4. |
| Observe, don't predict | No a priori job type or length. | Watch execution; adjust priority from behavior over time. |
| Real systems | MLFQ variants in production OSes. | BSD UNIX derivatives, Solaris, Windows NT and later Windows versions. |

## 2. Why MLFQ?

Ch. 7 left two goals in tension:

| Goal | Ideal policy | Problem |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Minimize turnaround | SJF / STCF | OS does not know job length in advance |
| Minimize response (interactive) | RR with small quantum | Poor turnaround for long jobs |

**MLFQ** uses multiple queues at different priority levels and **changes a job's
priority based on observed behavior** — interactive (I/O-heavy, short bursts) vs
CPU-bound (long runs without yielding).

Unlike SJF/STCF, it does **not** require **a priori** knowledge of whether a job
is short or long. The scheduler **observes how a job actually runs** and assigns
priority from that feedback.

---

## 3. Allotment

**Allotment** — the amount of time a job can spend at a given priority level
before the scheduler reduces its priority.

For simplicity, at first assume **allotment = a single time slice**.

---

## 4. Basic rules (Attempt #1)

**Scheduling among queues**

- **Rule 1:** If `Priority(A) > Priority(B)`, A runs (B doesn't).
- **Rule 2:** If `Priority(A) = Priority(B)`, A and B run in **RR**.

**How priority is assigned and changed**

- **Rule 3:** When a job enters the system, it is placed at the **highest priority** (the topmost queue).
- **Rule 4a:** If a job **uses up its allotment** while running, its priority is reduced (it moves down one queue).
- **Rule 4b:** If a job **gives up the CPU** (e.g., by performing an I/O operation) **before** the allotment is up, it **stays at the same priority level** (its allotment is reset).

```
High priority  Q_top  ──  new jobs, interactive work
               Q_mid
               Q_low  ──  proven CPU-bound jobs
Low priority
```

---

## 5. How MLFQ approximates SJF

MLFQ does not know whether a job is short or long. It **assumes it might be a
short job** and gives it **high priority** (Rule 3).

- If the job **is** short → it finishes quickly at the top queue (like SJF).
- If the job **is** long → it uses its allotment, gets demoted, and slowly sinks to lower queues — proving itself CPU-bound over time.

In this way, MLFQ **approximates SJF** without knowing run times in advance.

---

## 6. I/O and interactive jobs

When an **I/O interrupt** happens and the job relinquishes the CPU **before**
using its allotment, **Rule 4b** keeps it at the **same priority level** (allotment
reset).

Intent: interactive jobs that frequently wait on keyboard, disk, etc. should not
be penalized for being I/O-bound. They stay high in the MLFQ and get good
**response time** — similar to what RR provides for interactive work, without
giving every job an equal small quantum forever.

---

## 7. Problems: starvation and gaming

The basic MLFQ (Rules 1–4a/4b) has serious flaws.

**Starvation**

If too many interactive jobs stay at high priority, they can consume all CPU
time. Long-running CPU-bound jobs at the bottom may **never run**.

**Gaming the scheduler**

A process can **monopolize the CPU** under Rules 4a and 4b:

1. Run for ~99% of the allotment.
2. Issue an I/O operation and relinquish the CPU.
3. Stay at the same priority (Rule 4b); allotment resets.
4. Repeat.

The job keeps high priority while using most of each slice — unfair share of the CPU.

**Changing behavior**

A job may start CPU-bound and later become interactive. Without a boost, it
may remain stuck in a low queue even after its behavior changes.

---

## 8. Rule 5: priority boost

**Attempt #2** — add periodic reset:

- **Rule 5:** After some time period **S**, move **all jobs** in the system to the **topmost queue**.

This addresses two problems:

1. **Prevents starvation** — even low-priority jobs eventually reach the top and get CPU time via RR with other high-priority jobs.
2. **Adapts to behavior change** — if a CPU-bound job becomes interactive, it is treated properly once it receives the priority boost.

Choosing **S** is tricky (Ousterhout's "voodoo constant"): too large → starvation risk; too small → interactive jobs lose their advantage.

---

## 9. Better accounting (Attempt #3)

**Problem:** Rules 4a and 4b let a job **retain** priority by yielding before allotment expires — the gaming attack above.

**Fix:** track **total CPU time used at each level**, not just "did you yield in time?"

Replace Rule 4a and 4b with a single rule:

- **Rule 4:** Once a job uses up its **time allotment at a given level** (regardless of **how many times** it has given up the CPU), its priority is reduced (it moves down one queue).

Whether the job uses its allotment in one long burst or many small ones **does not matter** — I/O no longer resets demotion. Gaming is blocked; the job still slowly moves down if it is truly CPU-bound.

**Refined rule set (typical summary)**

| Rule | Content |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | Higher priority runs first. |
| 2 | Same priority → RR. |
| 3 | New job → top queue. |
| 4 | Allotment exhausted at current level → demote (total CPU at level, not per yield). |
| 5 | Every period S → boost all jobs to top queue. |

## 10. Best of both worlds and real systems

With the refined rules in place, MLFQ manages to achieve the **best of both
worlds**:

| Workload | What MLFQ delivers |
| --- | --- |
| Short-running **interactive** jobs | Excellent overall performance — response and turnaround similar to what **SJF/STCF** would give if run times were known |
| Long-running **CPU-intensive** jobs | **Fairness** and **progress** — lower priority and longer quanta, but Rule 5 prevents starvation; work still moves forward |

That combination — good interactive response without abandoning batch progress —
is why many real systems use a form of MLFQ as their **base scheduler**, including:

- **BSD UNIX derivatives** (e.g., FreeBSD)
- **Solaris** (Time-Sharing scheduling class)
- **Windows NT** and subsequent **Windows** operating systems

Exact queue counts, time slices, allotments, and boost intervals differ per OS,
but the core idea is the same: **learn from execution, prioritize accordingly.**

> **Final takeaway**
> MLFQ observes execution instead of demanding a priori knowledge: start optimistic
> (high priority), demote CPU hogs, boost periodically to avoid starvation and
> stale classification, and account for CPU time honestly so users cannot trick
> the scheduler. That yields SJF/STCF-like performance for interactive work and
> fair progress for long CPU-bound jobs — which is why BSD derivatives, Solaris,
> and Windows NT and later Windows versions build on MLFQ-style scheduling.
