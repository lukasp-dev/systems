# OSTEP — CPU Scheduling: Proportional Share (Ch. 9)

> *Based on notes from Operating Systems: Three Easy Pieces — Scheduling: Proportional Share*

> **Big picture**
> Proportional-share (a.k.a. **fair-share**) schedulers do **not** try to optimize
> turnaround or response time. Instead they guarantee that each job receives a
> **certain percentage of CPU time**.
> The classic example is **lottery scheduling**: hold a lottery every time slice
> to decide which job runs next. A job that should run more often simply holds
> **more tickets** and therefore has a higher chance of winning.
> Contrast with [01.multi_level_feedback_queue.md](./01.multi_level_feedback_queue.md):
> MLFQ learns from behavior to approximate SJF/RR; proportional share instead
> divides the CPU by an explicit **share** each job is entitled to.

## 1. One-page cheat sheet

| Topic | Meaning | Remember this |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Proportional share | Guarantee each job a % of CPU, not best turnaround/response. | Also called **fair-share** scheduling. |
| Ticket | A unit representing a job's **share** of a resource. | `share = your tickets / total tickets`. |
| Lottery | Pick a random winning ticket each slice; that job runs. | Uses **randomness**; correct *probabilistically* over time. |
| Ticket currency | A user allocates tickets to its own jobs in a local currency. | System converts currency → global tickets. |
| Ticket transfer | A process hands tickets to another (e.g. client → server). | Useful in client/server work. |
| Ticket inflation | A process temporarily raises its own ticket count. | Only safe among **trusting** processes. |
| Total tickets | Denominator of `share`; not always constant. | Currency & transfer **conserve** it; **inflation changes** it. |
| List order | Order of jobs in the lottery list. | Changes number→winner mapping & loop speed, **not** long-run share. |
| Stride | `stride = big# / tickets`; lowest **pass** value runs next. | **Deterministic** version of lottery. |
| Pass value | Per-job counter advanced by its stride when it runs. | Run job with min pass → add its stride. |
| Lottery advantage | No global state; new jobs join trivially. | Stride must initialize a new job's pass value. |
| CFS | Linux's fair-share scheduler (not ticket-based). | Uses **vruntime** + red-black tree; very efficient. |
| Nice value | −20…+19 priority; maps to a **weight**. | **Lower nice = higher priority**; vruntime grows inversely to weight. |
| Sleeping-job cost | Waking job's vruntime set to `min_vruntime`. | Stops monopoly, but frequent short sleepers lose fair share. |
| Unfairness metric (U) | `time first job finishes / time second job finishes`. | `U → 1` means perfectly fair. |

## 2. Why proportional share?

Earlier policies (SJF, STCF, RR, MLFQ) optimize **turnaround time** or **response
time**. Proportional-share schedulers ask a different question:

> Instead of optimizing turnaround or response, can we **guarantee that each job
> obtains a certain percentage of CPU time**?

This is the right goal when the resource is something users have *paid for* or
are *entitled to* — e.g. virtual machines on a shared host, or users sharing a
compute cluster.

**Lottery scheduling** (Waldspurger & Weihl) is the seminal example: hold a
lottery periodically to decide which process runs next; processes that should run
more often get **more chances to win**.

**How the pieces fit together.** Proportional share is the *goal*; lottery and
stride are two *mechanisms* that enforce it, and CFS is how Linux realizes the
same fairness idea in production:

```
                  Proportional-Share Scheduling
                  (goal: each job gets a % of CPU)
                              │
          ┌───────────────────┼───────────────────┐
          ▼                   ▼                   ▼
    Lottery             Stride              Linux CFS
   (randomized)      (deterministic)    (fair-share, no tickets)
          │
          ▼
   Ticket mechanisms
   currency / transfer / inflation
```

| Item | Role | Proportional share? |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Proportional share | The **goal/category** — guarantee each job its CPU share. | The umbrella concept itself. |
| **Lottery** | A **mechanism** to enforce it (randomized). | Yes — a concrete implementation. |
| **Stride** | Another **mechanism** (deterministic). | Yes — the exact alternative to lottery. |
| Ticket currency / transfer / inflation | **Sub-features of lottery** — ways to manipulate the tickets that express shares. | Yes, but *tools within* lottery, not standalone schedulers. |
| **CFS** | Linux's real **fair-share** scheduler (uses `vruntime`, not tickets). | Same goal, different technique — a close cousin. |

## 3. Lottery scheduling

**Lottery scheduling** is the original proportional-share mechanism: each job
holds tickets equal to its share, and a random draw each time slice picks the
winner. The rest of this section covers how tickets express shares, ways to
manipulate them, the (trivial) implementation, its probabilistic fairness, and
the open ticket-assignment problem.

### 3.1 Tickets represent your share

**Tickets** represent the **share** of a resource a process should receive.

> The percent of tickets a process holds = its **share** of the system resource.

**Example.** Two jobs, A and B:

- A has **75 tickets** (numbers 0–74).
- B has **25 tickets** (numbers 75–99).

The scheduler should give A **75%** of the CPU and B **25%**.

**How a lottery slice works**

1. The scheduler knows the **total** number of tickets (here 100).
2. It picks a **random winning number** in `[0, total)`.
3. Whichever job owns that ticket runs for this time slice.

```
Winning ticket → 63  → owned by A → A runs
Winning ticket → 85  → owned by B → B runs
```

Over many slices the **law of large numbers** drives the actual split toward
75/25. It is **probabilistic**, not exact on any single slice.

A possible run (A = job with more tickets):

```
A A A B A A A A A A B A A B A A A A A A ...
```

The longer it runs, the closer the proportions get to the desired 75/25.

### 3.2 Ticket mechanisms

Lottery scheduling gives several flexible ways to **manipulate tickets**.

| Mechanism | What it does | When it helps |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Ticket currency** | A user is given a set of tickets and may divide them among its own jobs in any **local currency**; the system converts that currency into the correct number of **global tickets**. | Lets users manage their own jobs' relative shares without affecting other users. |
| **Ticket transfer** | A process temporarily **hands its tickets to another** process. | A client transfers tickets to a server doing work on its behalf, so the server runs more to finish the client's request. |
| **Ticket inflation** | A process temporarily **raises (or lowers) its own** ticket count. | Among **mutually trusting** processes: one that needs more CPU can inflate without communicating with anyone. Dangerous with untrusted/greedy processes. |

**Ticket currency example.**
Users A and B each have **100 (global) tickets**.

- A runs two jobs, A1 and A2, and gives each **500 of A's own currency**.
- B runs one job, B1, with **10 of B's currency**.

The system converts each user's local allocation back to **100 global tickets**:

- A1 and A2 → **50 global tickets each** (A's 100 split evenly).
- B1 → **100 global tickets**.

Each user still controls only their own 100-ticket slice of the machine. The
local numbers (500, 10) are arbitrary — only the **ratio inside each currency**
matters, since the system re-normalizes back to the user's global share.

**Does the total ticket count stay fixed?** Only inflation can break it — an
important interview point. Recall `share = your tickets / total tickets`, so the
**denominator is not constant**:

| Mechanism | Total tickets change? | Why |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Ticket currency | **No** | Each user's local sum is normalized back to their fixed global share. |
| Ticket transfer | **No** | One process loses exactly what another gains — the **sum is conserved**. |
| **Ticket inflation** | **Yes** | A process raises its own count, enlarging the denominator and shrinking everyone else's share — which is exactly why it is only safe among **mutually trusting** processes. |

**Inflation example.** A, B, C each hold 100 (total 300, each = 1/3). If A
inflates to 400: total becomes **600**, so A's share jumps to `400/600 = 67%`
while B and C drop to `100/600 ≈ 17%` each. A greedy process could inflate without
bound and monopolize the CPU — hence the trust requirement.

### 3.3 Implementation: how simple it is

One of lottery scheduling's biggest selling points: **the implementation is
trivial**. You need:

1. A good **random number generator** to pick the winning ticket.
2. A **data structure** holding the processes (e.g. a list).
3. The **total** number of tickets.

**Algorithm (decide the winner each slice)**

```c
// counter: used to track if we've found the winner yet
int counter = 0;

// winner: call some random routine to get a value in [0, totaltickets)
int winner = getrandom(0, totaltickets);

// current: use this to walk through the list of jobs
node_t *current = head;

// loop until the sum of ticket values exceeds the winner
while (current) {
    counter = counter + current->tickets;
    if (counter > winner)
        break;            // found the winner
    current = current->next;
}
// 'current' is the winner: schedule it...
```

**Why it works — cumulative ranges.** Each job implicitly owns a **contiguous
range** of ticket numbers equal to its ticket count. Walking the list and summing
tickets finds the first job whose running total **passes** `winner` — that job owns
that number. For A=75, B=25:

```
job:      |<------- A (75) ------->|<-- B (25) -->|
number:    0                     74 75          99
winner=85 → counter: A→75 (75>85? no) → B→100 (100>85? yes) → B wins
```

**Does list order matter?** A subtle but classic interview point:

- **The number → winner mapping changes.** Reordering the list reassigns which
  *specific* numbers belong to which job, so the **same** `winner` can pick a
  **different** job. (With `[C,A,B]`, 85 → C; with `[B,A,C]`, 85 → A.)
- **The long-run share does *not* change.** Each job still owns exactly `tickets`
  worth of numbers (just a different block), and the random draw is uniform, so
  `P(win) = tickets / total` is **independent of order**. Over many slices every
  ordering converges to the same proportions.
- Lottery also has **no fixed rotation order** — each slice is an *independent*
  draw, unlike stride's deterministic sequence.

**Optimization.** Keep the list **sorted from most tickets to fewest**. Order does
not change correctness or shares, but putting high-ticket jobs first **minimizes
the number of list iterations** needed to find the winner in the common case.
Each slice re-runs from scratch: `counter` and `current` are reset every draw;
only each job's win count persists.

### 3.4 Fairness and randomness

Because lottery uses randomness, fairness is only **probabilistic** — and it
depends on how long the jobs run.

**Unfairness metric (U)** — for two jobs of the same length:

$$U = \frac{\text{time the first job completes}}{\text{time the second job completes}}$$

- If both finish at nearly the same time, `U` → **1** (perfectly fair).
- If one finishes much earlier, `U` is small (unfair).

**Example.** Two jobs, each of length 10, equal tickets.

- If the first finishes at time 10 and the second at time 20: `U = 10/20 = 0.5`.
- If both finish near time 20: `U ≈ 20/20 = 1`.

**Key insight:** the **longer the jobs run**, the closer `U` gets to 1. With short
jobs, a few unlucky lottery draws can cause noticeable unfairness. Randomness only
balances out **over time** (law of large numbers).

> Randomness gives lottery three nice properties:
> 1. It avoids **worst-case** corner cases that haunt deterministic policies.
> 2. It is **lightweight** — almost no per-job state to track.
> 3. It is **fast** — just generate a number and walk the list.

### 3.5 The ticket-assignment problem

A real open problem: **how should tickets be assigned to jobs?**

> The system's behavior is strongly dependent on how tickets are allocated, yet
> there is **no single best way** to assign them.

The scheduler simply assumes the user knows the right shares and assigns tickets
accordingly — but in practice users rarely know. This is the
**ticket-assignment problem**, and lottery scheduling does not solve it; it just
provides the **mechanism** to enforce whatever shares are chosen.

## 4. Stride scheduling

Randomness makes lottery simple but only **probabilistically** correct. **Stride
scheduling** is a **deterministic** fair-share scheduler that gets the proportions
right *exactly*.

**Definitions**

- Each job has a **stride**: `stride = (large number) / (number of tickets)`.
  - The large number is arbitrary; OSTEP uses **10000**.
- Each job has a **pass** value, starting at 0.
- The bigger your ticket count, the **smaller** your stride, so your pass grows
  slowly → you are picked more often.

**Algorithm**

1. Pick the job with the **lowest pass** value to run.
2. After it runs one slice, increment its pass by its **stride**.
3. Repeat.

```c
current = remove_min(queue);     // pick client with min pass value
schedule(current);               // run it for one quantum
current->pass += current->stride;  // update pass using its stride
insert(queue, current);          // put it back
```

**Worked example** — A=100, B=50, C=250 tickets. Strides = 10000 / tickets:

| Job | Tickets | Stride |
| --- | --- | --- |
| A | 100 | 100 |
| B | 50  | 200 |
| C | 250 | 40  |

Trace (run the **min pass**, then add that job's stride; tie → break by order):

| Pass(A) | Pass(B) | Pass(C) | Who ran? |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 0   | 0   | 0   | A |
| 100 | 0   | 0   | B |
| 100 | 200 | 0   | C |
| 100 | 200 | 40  | C |
| 100 | 200 | 80  | C |
| 100 | 200 | 120 | A |
| 200 | 200 | 120 | C |
| 200 | 200 | 160 | C |
| 200 | 200 | 200 | … |

Count the runs in one full cycle: **C ran 5×, A ran 2×, B ran 1×** — exactly the
250 : 100 : 50 = 5 : 2 : 1 ratio. Stride hits the target **precisely**, not just on
average.

## 5. Lottery vs stride: why lottery still matters

If stride is exact, why keep lottery? Because of **global state**.

| Aspect | Lottery | Stride |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Correctness | Probabilistic (right over time). | Deterministic (right exactly each cycle). |
| Per-job state | **None** beyond ticket count. | Must track each job's **pass** value. |
| **New job joining** | Trivial — just add its tickets. | **Hard** — what pass value do you give it? |

**The new-job problem.** If a fresh job enters stride scheduling with **pass = 0**,
it has the minimum pass and will **monopolize the CPU** until its pass catches up
to the others. Stride must carefully initialize the newcomer's pass (e.g. to the
current minimum).

Lottery has **no global state per process**, so a new job simply joins with its
tickets and the next lottery naturally accounts for it. That simplicity is
lottery's lasting advantage.

## 6. The Linux Completely Fair Scheduler (CFS)

Modern Linux takes the fair-share idea but implements it for **efficiency and
scalability** — the **Completely Fair Scheduler (CFS)**. It is *not* ticket-based.

**Core ideas**

- **vruntime (virtual runtime):** each running process accumulates `vruntime` as
  it runs. CFS always picks the process with the **lowest vruntime** next — the
  one that has had the least CPU so far.
- **sched_latency / min_granularity:** CFS computes each process's time slice from
  a target latency divided across runnable processes, with a floor
  (`min_granularity`) so slices never get too tiny when many jobs are runnable.
- **niceness / weights:** the classic UNIX **nice** value (−20 … +19, default 0)
  maps to a **weight**. **Lower nice = higher priority** (−20 is strongest); a
  *higher* nice value means "nicer" → yields more CPU. `vruntime` advances
  **inversely** to weight:

  $$\text{vruntime} \mathrel{+}= \text{actual runtime} \times \frac{weight_0}{weight_i}$$

  High-priority (low nice → large weight) → vruntime grows **slowly** → stays the
  minimum longer → runs more often. Same real time elapses for everyone; priority
  only changes **how fast vruntime accrues**, which is what produces the
  proportional share.
- **Red-black tree:** runnable processes are kept in a balanced **red-black tree**
  ordered by `vruntime`. Finding the next job (leftmost node) and inserting/removing
  are `O(log n)` — efficient even with thousands of processes. Sleeping processes
  are removed from the tree.

**The sleeping-job cost.** While a job sleeps it accrues **no** vruntime, so it
falls far behind. To stop a long-sleeping job from waking up and **monopolizing**
the CPU (its tiny vruntime would keep it minimum for a long time), CFS sets a
waking job's vruntime to the tree's current **minimum (`min_vruntime`)**. This
prevents starvation of others, **but comes at a cost**: a job that **sleeps for
short periods frequently** has the small credit it earns wiped out on every wake,
so it **never gets its fair share** of the CPU.

**Why it matters:** CFS shows the proportional-share idea scaled to a real,
high-performance OS — fairness via `vruntime` accounting instead of literal
lotteries or stride passes.

## 7. Summary and when to use it

| When proportional share shines | When it is awkward |
| --- | --- |
| You care about **guaranteed CPU shares** (VMs, paying users, clusters). | You need optimal **turnaround/response** for unknown-length jobs. |
| Mechanism to **enforce** chosen shares (tickets / strides / vruntime). | You must decide *what* the shares should be — the unsolved **ticket-assignment problem**. |
| **I/O-light**, CPU-bound, easily divisible workloads. | Jobs do lots of **I/O** — they may not use their full share. |

**Takeaways**

- **Lottery** = randomized, dead-simple, probabilistically fair, trivial to add
  jobs; correctness improves the longer jobs run.
- **Stride** = deterministic, exact proportions, but needs per-job global state
  and careful handling of newcomers.
- **CFS** = Linux's efficient, non-ticket fair-share scheduler using `vruntime` and
  a red-black tree.
- Proportional share is **not** the default general-purpose scheduler because
  (a) the I/O problem and (b) the ticket-assignment problem make it best for
  domains where shares are known and meaningful (e.g. virtualized environments).

> **Final takeaway**
> Proportional-share scheduling trades turnaround/response optimization for an
> explicit guarantee: every job gets the **share** it is owed. Lottery enforces
> this with randomness (simple, stateless, probabilistically fair), stride enforces
> it deterministically (exact, but needs global pass state), and Linux's CFS
> realizes the same fairness goal efficiently with `vruntime` and a red-black tree.
> The mechanism is solid; the hard part it leaves open is deciding **what each
> job's share should be** — the ticket-assignment problem.
